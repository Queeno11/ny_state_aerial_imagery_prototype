\section{Computer Vision and Mixed Spatial-Temporal Siamese Architecture}

To capture the full economic dynamics of the urban environment while ensuring strict out-of-sample generalizability across diverse urban typologies, our architecture is explicitly designed with a \textbf{dual objective}. First, it acts as an ordinal feature extractor to predict the relative ranking of neighborhood structural wealth at a given point in time. Second, it predicts the directional magnitude of structural change (gentrification or decay) across a multi-year panel. 

To achieve this without suffering from spatial cardinality mismatch or catastrophic forgetting, we employ a Mixed Spatial-Temporal Siamese framework. This architecture utilizes stochastic batch composition to simultaneously map the static visual hierarchy of wealth and regularize the network against temporal survey noise.

\subsection{Feature Extraction and Spatial Spillover ($\tau$)}
To isolate the temporal learning mechanism from extraneous optical noise (e.g., building shadows, changes in sun angles and lighting), the prototype model utilizes rasterized vector building footprints sourced from the official New York City Department of Information Technology and Telecommunications (DoITT). 

Because neighborhood wealth is influenced by localized externalities, the visual input for tract $i$ is not strictly bounded by its census geometry. We introduce a \textbf{spatial spillover hyperparameter, $\tau$}, defined as a buffer radius (in meters) extending beyond each building centroid. Let $X_{i,t}^\tau$ represent the multi-channel rasterized matrix of tract $i$ in year $t \in \{2008, 2010, 2012, 2014, 2016, 2018, 2020, 2022, 2024\}$ inclusive of the $\tau$-meter spillover, encoding the features latent in the aerial imagery.

We introduce a \textbf{spatial spillover hyperparameter, $\tau$}, defined as a buffer radius (in meters) extending beyond each building centroid. Let $X_{b, i,t}^\tau$ represent the multi-channel matrix of building $b$ in tract $i$ in year $t \in \{2008, 2010, 2012, 2014, 2016, 2018, 2020, 2022, 2024\}$ inclusive of the $\tau$-meter spillover, encoding the features latent in the aerial imagery. 

We define a feature extraction sub-network, $f_\theta(\cdot)$, parameterized by weights $\theta$. This sub-network projects the physical infrastructure matrix into a lower-dimensional latent representation, $h_{b,i,t} \in \mathbb{R}^d$:
\begin{equation}
h_{b,i,t} = f_\theta(X_{b,i,t}^\tau)
\end{equation}

A fully connected regression head, $g_\phi(\cdot)$ with weights $\phi$, then maps this physical embedding to a continuous scalar ranking score, representing the tract's relative structural wealth position:
\begin{equation}
\hat{R}_{b,i,t} = g_\phi(h_{b,i,t})
\end{equation}

Inside each tract $i$, every building $b$ has the same relative rank $\hat{R}_{b,i,t} = \hat{R}_{i,t}$. We exploit the fact that there is a strong spatial correlation in building morphology within a tract, and interpret the differences between the real unobserved ranking and the ground truth ranking as measurement errors. Because the model is never feed with more than a few buildings per tract per epoch, it is forced to learn the underlying structural hierarchy of wealth without overfitting to the visual noise of individual buildings.

\subsection{Out-of-Sample Generalizability and Quantile Mapping}
A core methodological innovation of this architecture is the intentional discarding of spatial cardinality during cross-sectional training. Urban topologies scale differently; a one-standard-deviation increase in structural wealth in New York manifests vertically, whereas in Dallas, it manifests horizontally. By constraining the network to act strictly as a monotonic ordinal ranker within a specific city's batch, we prevent the model from overfitting to the topological density of the training data. 

To deploy this model in a purely out-of-sample city (e.g., Boston), we compute the continuous ranking scores ($\hat{R}$) for all target tracts based purely on their physical morphology. We then compute their empirical quantiles and map them directly to the corresponding quantiles of the target city's actual American Community Survey (ACS) income distribution, allowing for zero-shot econometric moment matching.

\subsection{Stochastic Batch Composition}
We utilize a stochastic weighting strategy via batch composition. Each training batch of size $B$ is explicitly stratified into three pair types to force the network to learn a universal ordinal hierarchy:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Spatial Pairs (80\%):} Pairs of distinct buildings drawn from different tracts but within the same city and year $(X_{b,i,t}^\tau, X_{c,j,t}^\tau)$. To prevent the network from overfitting to visual noise within identical socio-economic contexts, spatial pairs are strictly constrained to distinct GEOIDs, ensuring the model assesses structural gradients across genuine geographic bounds.
    \item \textbf{Stable Temporal Pairs (10\%):} Pairs of the \textit{same} physical building structure across time $(X_{b,i,t_a}^\tau, X_{b,i,t_b}^\tau)$ where the econometrically cleared Macro Gate indicates no structural change ($I_i^{valid} = 0$). Crucially, for these pairs, the network ignores the natural temporal drift in their relative cross-sectional Z-scores (economic filtering) and exclusively penalizes divergence in their latent physical embeddings.
    \item \textbf{Changing Temporal Pairs (10\%):} Pairs of the \textit{same} physical building structure across time $(X_{b,i,t_a}^\tau, X_{b,i,t_b}^\tau)$ where valid structural change occurred ($I_i^{valid} = 1$).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Unified Pairwise Difference Regression Loss}
To ensure out-of-sample generalization by structurally discarding spatial cardinality, we frame the objective as a continuous relative difference problem \cite{parikh2011relative}. We train the network to minimize the pairwise squared error between predicted and true relative score differences \cite{daudt2018fully, chen2021remote, burges2005learning}. This unifies the cross-sectional ranking and temporal change objectives under a single differentiable loss function that is translation-invariant by construction.

For any pair of observations $u$ and $v$ drawn from our stochastically composed batch $\mathbb{B}$, let $\hat{R}_u$ and $\hat{R}_v$ be their predicted scalar scores, and $L_u$ and $L_v$ be their true year-specific ACS econometric labels. The unified pairwise loss is defined as:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{pair}(\theta, \phi) = \frac{1}{|\mathbb{B}|} \sum_{(u, v) \in \mathbb{B}} \left[ \left(\hat{R}_{u} - \hat{R}_{v}\right) - \left(L_{u} - L_{v}\right) \right]^2
\end{equation}

This single formulation gracefully handles all three pair types in our stochastic batch composition natively:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Spatial Pairs:} The target is $L_i - L_j$. The model learns to reproduce the full continuous magnitude of cross-sectional wealth differences rather than just their directional ranking, effectively discarding absolute topological anchors while capturing nuanced relative hierarchies.
    \item \textbf{Stable Temporal Pairs:} The target is explicitly forced to $0$. Because a deterministic CNN must output identical scalars for identical physical inputs, supervising on the noisy, non-zero ACS economic drift would induce gradient gridlock. By setting the target difference to zero, the pairwise formulation elegantly simplifies into a Siamese robustness penalty $(R_{b,i,t_b} - R_{b,i,t_a})^2$, forcing the model to strictly ignore transient survey noise.
    \item \textbf{Changing Temporal Pairs:} The target is the intertemporal difference $L_{i,t_b} - L_{i,t_a}$. Because MSE penalizes missed magnitudes quadratically, it provides a strong, natural gradient signal proportional to the severity of the structural change, removing the need for an engineered dynamic margin.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Summary of Core Architectural Hyperparameters}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{$\tau$ (Spillover Threshold):} Determines the spatial context window (in meters) beyond the building centroid to capture localized externalities.
    \item \textbf{Batch Split $(\rho_{spatial}, \rho_{stable}, \rho_{change})$:} Set to (0.8, 0.1, 0.1) to define the stochastic probability of drawing a cross-sectional comparison versus temporal regularization pairs.
\end{itemize}
